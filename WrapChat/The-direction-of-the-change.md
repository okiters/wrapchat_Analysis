The main idea I would keep is this: the reports should not only tell users what happened; they should make users confront what they assumed happened. That is exactly why the new Guess, Quiz, and Who Said This mechanics are promising. Your document already frames this correctly: the cards stop being pure “delivery” and become “exchanges,” which changes the experience even if the number of cards stays similar.
My genuine verdict
I would not switch the new cards with something completely else. I would keep the direction, but be stricter about which cards deserve to exist.
The strongest part of the redesign is not “more cards.” It is that each report now has moments of commitment:
“Who do you think it is?”
“Who said this?”
“Do you actually remember this relationship correctly?”
“Does the other person see the same thing?”
That is much more engaging than passive stats.
The weak risk is that you may be over-expanding everything to 10 cards because it feels premium. I agree that 5-card paid reports feel thin, especially next to a 16-card General Wrapped. But 10 should not become a rule just because it looks balanced. The better rule is: paid reports should feel complete, but not padded. Your own analysis says no paid report should feel like it ends before the user is ready; I agree with that principle.
So: keep the concept, but validate the card count per report.
The new card types: keep or cut?
1. Guess Before Reveal — Keep. Very strong.
This is probably the best core interaction. It is not just a quiz; it creates psychological investment before the result. Your document makes the right distinction: a quiz tests knowledge, but a prediction makes the user commit to a belief before the data corrects them.
This is exactly the kind of mechanic that can make people say:
“Wait, I thought it was me.”
That reaction is valuable.
But I would be careful with frequency. Guess cards should only appear when the answer is:
emotionally interesting,
not obvious,
clearly skewed enough to reveal.
Your “balanced-data rule” is very important. If the answer is close to 50/50, do not force a guess. That would feel fake and annoying.
Best places for Guess cards:
Ghost Award / reply time
Love Language
Who apologizes more
Who changed more
Who holds the power
Weaker places:
Who made more promises
Who sent longer messages
Who reaches out first, unless the difference is dramatic
So I would keep Guess cards, but treat them as premium interaction moments, not a default wrapper for any comparison.
2. Who Said This? — Keep. Possibly the strongest card emotionally.
This is the card type with the highest “this app actually read my chat” feeling.
AI summaries can feel generic. A real message cannot. When the app shows a hidden message and asks “Who said this?”, the user immediately recognizes the emotional texture of the relationship.
Your reveal standard is also right: attribution alone is not enough. The card needs the sender, timestamp, and what was happening around the message. Without that third part, it is just a guessing game. With it, it becomes a remembered moment.
I would use this card in almost every major paid report, but not too many times in one report.
Strongest uses:
Love Language: Most Loving Moment
Toxicity: conflict message
Growth: message near the turning point
Accountability: promise that changed things
General Wrapped: funny/distinctive message
The only caution: in Toxicity, this card can become too intense. A conflict quote shown out of context could feel accusatory or even unsafe. Your idea to include “What’s Still Here” before the final verdict is smart because it prevents the report from feeling like a pure indictment.
Keep it, but build strong safety rules. Avoid surfacing messages involving threats, self-harm, sexual pressure, severe abuse, or highly private trauma as playful attribution cards. Those should trigger softer framing or be excluded.
3. Chat Memory Quiz — Keep, but build later than the report cards.
The quiz is your best growth mechanic. It is much better than “share screenshot,” because it creates a loop: one person sends the quiz, the other person scores, then they want to compare or generate their own result. Your document describes that loop clearly.
But I would not build it before the report experience itself is strong.
The quiz depends on the reports feeling accurate and emotionally satisfying. If the report feels generic, the quiz will feel like a gimmick. If the report feels personal, the quiz becomes a natural extension.
Your idea that quiz questions should mostly come from computed stats is also correct. If the recipient has not generated the report, the quiz cannot depend on fresh AI calls.
My recommendation:
Phase 1: Build Guess + Attribution inside reports.
Phase 2: Build Finale with sharing.
Phase 3: Add Chat Memory Quiz after you know which report cards users actually enjoy.

What I would change in your current plan
1. Do not make every report exactly 10 cards by force
10 is a good target, but not sacred.
A better structure:
Report	Recommended target
Quick Read	8
General Wrapped Duo	15–16
General Wrapped Group	16
Love Language	9–10
Energy	9–10
Toxicity	8–10
Accountability	8–9
Growth	10
The biggest danger is padding Accountability and Toxicity. The biggest opportunity is Growth and Love Language.
2. Create a “proof strength” system internally
Some cards should only appear if the data is strong enough.
For example:
Guess card only appears if the winner is meaningfully ahead.
Attribution card only appears if the selected message is clear, safe, and context-rich.
Turning Point only appears if there is a detectable temporal shift.
Love Language Miss only appears if there is an identifiable mismatch moment.
Power Balance only appears if evidence is strong enough; otherwise show “The Balance Is Mixed.”
This avoids generic or fake-feeling results.
3. Be careful with AI overconfidence
Your app is analyzing chats, not diagnosing relationships. Especially in Toxicity, Power, Love Language, and Accountability, the copy should avoid absolute claims.
Use:
“seems”
“leans”
“in this chat”
“from the messages available”
“the pattern suggests”
Avoid:
“you are”
“they always”
“this proves”
“the truth is”
The product can still feel magical without pretending to be clinically certain.
4. Prioritize temporal cards
Time is underused and you are right to add it.
The best proposed cards are often time-based:
Time of Day
Energy by Time
Reliability Arc
Turning Point
Message That Shifted Everything
Love Language Over Time
These are strong because time makes the report feel less like a static personality quiz and more like a story.
